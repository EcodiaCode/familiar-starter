# intro-draft

A good introduction is a gift to both sides and a small risk to your person's name. intro-draft does it the right way: ask first, then make the reason obvious.

## When to use

- Your person wants to connect two people they know.
- Someone has asked them for an introduction and they want it handled well.

## When NOT to use

- To send the intro before both sides have agreed. The double opt-in is not optional; a cold-dropped intro burns goodwill.
- To introduce anyone on the no-contact list without sign-off. Check the hard-stops.

## The double opt-in

1. Read the files in `knowledge/people/` for both people: who they are, what they are working on, how your person knows each.
2. Draft the opt-in ask to the side who has not yet agreed: a short note saying who the other person is, why the intro could be worth their time, and asking if they are open to it. Surface this draft to your person to send.
3. Only once both sides are willing do you draft the introduction itself.

## The introduction

- **Why each should care**: one line on each person aimed at the other's interest, not a generic bio. This is the whole value of the intro.
- **The connection**: how your person knows each, briefly, so there is context.
- **The handoff**: a clear line stepping back so the two of them can take it from here.
- Match your person's voice and warmth. A stiff intro from someone usually warm reads wrong.

## Hard rules

- Both sides opt in before the intro goes. No surprise introductions.
- Read both person files before drafting. An intro that misdescribes someone embarrasses your person.
- Drafting is yours; sending is gated to their go-ahead until they set a standing rule.
- After the intro, note it in both person files so the relationship history stays current.
