# newsletter-draft

A newsletter that ships on time beats a perfect one that never goes out. doc-draft handles one-off documents; this is the recurring rhythm of a regular send.

## When to use

- A regular newsletter or update they send to a list.
- They ask for an issue drafted from what has happened lately.

## When NOT to use

- A one-off announcement; use doc-draft for that.
- To send to the list. Drafting is yours; pressing send to a mailing list is theirs until they set a standing rule.

## Gather the material

1. Read the last few issues to learn the structure, the tone, and what the audience expects. Match it.
2. Pull the raw material: recent episodes, the status board, anything they flagged as "worth putting in the newsletter".
3. Read `voice/profile.md`. A newsletter is their voice at length, so this matters more here than almost anywhere.
4. Confirm the one thing that should anchor the issue if it is not obvious.

## Draft the issue

- **Subject line**: write three options. The subject is most of whether it gets opened, so spend real effort here.
- **Opening**: a warm, specific first line. No "hope you're well" filler.
- **Body**: the two or three things worth their reader's time, each with a clear point. Cite anything load-bearing or mark it `UNVERIFIED:`.
- **One clear ask or link** if the issue has one. Not five competing calls to action.
- **Sign-off** in their actual style.

## Deliver

- Show the full draft inline plus the three subject options.
- For the send itself, walk the channel per the cdp-usage skill if it lives in a web tool with no API, and confirm the schedule or send took before claiming it.

## Hard rules

- Read past issues and the voice profile before drafting. A newsletter that breaks the established rhythm reads as off.
- No invented facts, no padding. A short honest issue beats a long thin one.
- Sending to the list is gated to their approval until they say otherwise. A bad send to a whole list cannot be unsent.
