# proposal-draft

A proposal is where work becomes money. A sharp one wins the job and sets the terms; a vague one invites scope creep and a haggle.

## When to use

- They describe a job to quote and want a proposal or scope drafted.
- A repeat client where a prior proposal can be the template.

## When NOT to use

- Before the scope is actually clear. A proposal built on a fuzzy brief sets a price you will regret. Pin the open questions first.
- To send anything. Pricing and terms are theirs to approve; you draft, they decide and send.

## Before you draft

1. Read the client file in `knowledge/people/` or `knowledge/clients/` for history, prior pricing, and how they buy.
2. Read the status board for the live state of this relationship.
3. Pull any past proposals to this client; match the structure they have already accepted.
4. Confirm the inputs you cannot guess: the deliverables, the timeline, and the price. If price is your person's call, ask for it or propose a range with your reasoning, marked clearly as a proposal not a fact.

## The structure

- **The problem, in their words**: open by showing you understood what they need. One short paragraph.
- **What we will deliver**: the specific deliverables, concrete and bounded. This is the line that prevents scope creep, so make it exact.
- **Timeline**: realistic milestones, not optimistic ones.
- **Price and terms**: the number, what it includes, what it does not, payment terms. Name what is out of scope explicitly so there is no later argument.
- **Next step**: one clear action to accept.

## Hard rules

- Price, terms, and anything legally binding are your person's decision. Draft them, flag them clearly as needing sign-off, never commit on their behalf.
- Be exact about inclusions and exclusions. Vagueness in a proposal is unbilled work later.
- Read the client history before drafting; a proposal that ignores prior terms looks careless.
- The proposal stays a draft until they approve every number and send it themselves.
