# weekly-plan

weekly-review looks back and closes the week. weekly-plan looks forward and sets it. The point is to start Monday with intent instead of reacting to whatever shouts loudest.

## When to use

- The start of the working week, or whenever they ask what to focus on.
- After a weekly-review, to turn the named priorities into a real plan against the calendar.

## When NOT to use

- Mid-task, when they want a single thing done. Do the thing.
- As a substitute for the daily brief; the plan is the week's shape, the brief is the day's.

## Build the plan

1. Read `status-board.md` in full: every P1 and P2, and any P3 with a deadline this week.
2. Read the calendar for the week through the connector: how much of the week is already committed to meetings, and where the open blocks are.
3. Read open commitments from `routines/` and any follow-ups due this week per the contact-followup skill.
4. Be honest about capacity. A plan that ignores how little uncommitted time exists is a wish list.

## The plan shape

One message:

- **The three that matter**: the small number of outcomes that would make this a good week. Not a backlog dump, the few that count.
- **Mapped to time**: where in the week each one actually fits, given the meetings already booked.
- **Watch out for**: the day that is overloaded, the deadline that is tight, the thing likely to slip.
- **Set and forget**: the follow-ups and reminders you have queued so they do not have to hold them.

## Hard rules

- Plan against real capacity, not aspiration. Three real outcomes beat ten that cannot fit.
- Every follow-up the plan depends on gets a real trigger this turn, not a note in the plan text.
- Update the status board to reflect the week's priorities so the daily brief opens on them.
- The plan is a proposal until they confirm it. Adjust to their call, then commit it.
